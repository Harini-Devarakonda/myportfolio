# Stakeholder Management: A Business Analyst's Playbook

*By Sai Harini Devarakonda | January 2025*

---

## Introduction

Poor stakeholder management is one of the top causes of project failure. Missed requirements, resistant users, delayed approvals, and scope creep almost always trace back to stakeholders who were not identified, understood, or engaged effectively. This playbook gives Business Analysts a practical, step-by-step approach to managing stakeholders throughout the full project lifecycle.

---

## Step 1: Identify All Stakeholders

Start broader than you think you need to. Missed stakeholders surface late, and late surprises derail projects.

**Where to look:**
- Organisational charts — who owns the impacted business areas?
- Existing process documentation — who currently performs the process?
- Regulatory and compliance teams — who must sign off on changes?
- IT/infrastructure teams — who will be affected by technical changes?
- End users — who will use the new system or process daily?
- Supplier and partner organisations — who delivers inputs to the process?
- Customer or patient groups — who is the ultimate beneficiary or risk-bearer?

**Stakeholder register fields:**
```
Name | Role | Organisation | Contact | Interest | Influence | Attitude | Engagement approach
```

---

## Step 2: Map Stakeholders — Power / Interest Grid

Once identified, map stakeholders on a **Power / Interest Grid**:

```
High Power │ MANAGE CLOSELY  │  KEY PLAYERS
           │ (keep satisfied) │ (involve heavily)
           ├─────────────────┼──────────────────
Low Power  │  MONITOR        │  KEEP INFORMED
           │ (minimal effort) │ (regular updates)
           └─────────────────┴──────────────────
              Low Interest         High Interest
```

**Key Players** (high power, high interest): Sponsor, project board, senior business owners — engage them in requirements workshops, sprint reviews, and decision points.

**Keep Satisfied** (high power, low interest): Senior executives, regulatory bodies — keep them informed at milestones; avoid surprises.

**Keep Informed** (low power, high interest): End users, operational teams — involve them in UAT, pilot feedback, change communications.

**Monitor** (low power, low interest): Peripheral stakeholders — standard communications only.

---

## Step 3: Understand What Each Stakeholder Needs

Before any workshop or interview, understand:

1. **Their objective** — what outcome do they need from this project?
2. **Their concern** — what is their biggest risk or fear?
3. **Their influence** — can they block decisions or fund changes?
4. **Their availability** — are they constrained by competing priorities?
5. **Their preferred communication style** — detailed reports vs. high-level dashboards vs. verbal briefings?

The best way to gather this is through a **30-minute informal pre-engagement conversation** before the official project begins. People are more candid outside of formal sessions.

---

## Step 4: Design Your Elicitation Approach

Match your elicitation technique to the stakeholder type and information needed:

| Technique | Best For | BA Tips |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **1-to-1 Interviews** | Key decision-makers, sensitive topics | Prepare open-ended questions; record (with consent) for accuracy |
| **Workshops** | Cross-functional requirements, process mapping | Use Miro or physical whiteboards; set clear objectives upfront |
| **Surveys / Questionnaires** | Large user groups, prioritisation exercises | Keep under 10 minutes; use scales not free text where possible |
| **Observation (shadowing)** | Understanding what people actually do vs. what they say they do | Book time with frontline staff — their workarounds reveal real requirements |
| **Document Review** | Understanding current process and policy | Look for gaps between documented process and observed reality |
| **Focus Groups** | User experience, change readiness, piloting | Keep groups to 5–8 participants; use a skilled facilitator |

---

## Step 5: Run Effective Workshops

Workshops are the BA's highest-value activity — and the most commonly wasted hour. Make them count:

**Before the workshop:**
- Send a pre-read: scope, objectives, and what you need from participants
- Prepare a structured agenda with time-boxed activities
- Pre-populate any templates (process maps, user journey grids) to give participants a starting point

**During the workshop:**
- Open with: *"The objective today is [X]. We will have succeeded if we leave with [Y]."*
- Use a parking lot for off-topic items — validate them later rather than letting them derail the session
- Capture decisions separately from discussion — not everything said is a decision
- Close with agreed actions: who, what, when

**After the workshop:**
- Circulate notes within 24 hours
- List agreed actions with owners
- Flag any open questions with a response-by date

## Step 6: Manage Difficult Stakeholders

Every project has them. Common types and approaches:

| Stakeholder Type | Behaviour | Approach |
|-----------------|-----------|----------|
| The Blocker | Delays decisions, withholds approvals | Surface blockers explicitly in governance; escalate with evidence, not complaint |
| The Scope Creeper | Constantly adds new requirements | Acknowledge the idea, log it for future phases, refer back to agreed scope document |
| The Disengaged | Misses meetings, doesn't review documents | Find out why — usually workload or disinterest in the process. Find what *does* motivate them (outcome, not process). |
| The Micromanager | Wants to control every decision | Give them structured touchpoints (weekly updates, clear decision points) to reduce anxiety |
| The Resistant User | Doesn't want the change | Involve them early in design; make their expertise visible; address specific concerns directly |

**Golden rule:** Never let a difficult stakeholder relationship become adversarial. If it's heading that way, involve your sponsor early — not as a complaint, but as a risk to escalate.

---

## Step 7: Communication Planning

Document how, when, and what you will communicate to each stakeholder group:

```
Stakeholder Group | Information Needed | Format | Frequency | Owner
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Steering Group    | RAG status, risks  | 1-page report | Monthly | PM
Project Team      | Sprint progress    | Stand-up + board | Daily | BA/PM
End Users         | Change impact      | Email + team brief | Key milestones | BA
Regulators        | Compliance status  | Formal submission | Per requirement | PM/Legal
```

Adjust communication intensity as the project moves through phases — more frequent near delivery and go-live.

---

## Key Takeaway

Stakeholder management is not a soft skill — it is a **structured discipline** that determines whether requirements are complete, changes are accepted, and projects are delivered. BAs who invest in stakeholder relationships from day one spend less time managing crises at the end.

The most important thing you can do at the start of any project: **talk to people before you start taking requirements**. Understand their world, their pressures, and what success means to them. Everything else flows from that.
